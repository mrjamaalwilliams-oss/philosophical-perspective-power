Are We Building the Very Society History Warned Us About?

By Jamaal Williams • Democracy, Power & Society • May 2026

Movies like The Handmaid’s Tale and The Hunger Games may look like entertainment, but they also function as warnings about what can happen when power, wealth, and influence become concentrated in the hands of the few while ordinary people grow distracted, divided, or economically trapped.

Every generation in America has faced its own defining struggle, from the Revolutionary War and Civil Rights Movement to the Great Depression and world wars, and the question of this generation may be whether democracy can survive if corporations, wealthy interests, and powerful individuals gain more influence than everyday citizens.

A government of the people, by the people, and for the people cannot continue surviving if opportunity, education, housing, healthcare, and freedom increasingly become privileges reserved for the wealthy while millions struggle simply to remain stable.

If America truly wants a stronger future, society must fight ignorance with education, fear with unity, and inequality with real opportunity before the country slowly drifts toward the very kind of divided class system history has repeatedly warned about.

Education Is Not Up for Debate

Education has always evolved, but the larger principle remains the same: every person deserves access to learning because a society becomes stronger when more people are taught to think, question, reason, and solve problems.

The debate should not be whether people should be educated; the real debate is how, where, when, and in what ways education should prepare people for the world they are living in.

Should We Only Punish Crime, Or Prevent It?

As a criminologist, the question is not simply whether punishment is fair, but whether society is truly trying to understand why harmful behavior happens in the first place and how to prevent the next victim from ever existing.

The goal is not to excuse harmful actions, but to ask harder questions about behavior, prevention, accountability, and whether systems are healing society or reacting after the damage is already done.

Faith, Society & Leadership

When You Have the Anointing, But Not the Authority

The story of David and Saul in 1 Samuel shows one of the most complex places a gifted person can stand: carrying the anointing, influence, and capacity to lead while still lacking the title, office, or official authority.

David’s restraint matters because he had opportunities to harm Saul, yet chose conviction, humility, timing, and reverence over revenge, showing that true leadership is also about carrying power without being corrupted by it.

Community & Society

Political Visibility Without Economic Power Is Not Enough

When you look around New Orleans, different communities are building economic motion through restaurants, stores, gas stations, construction, beauty businesses, property ownership, and service-based industries, which raises a serious question for Black communities: why are Black people so often the consumers, but not always the owners?

This is not about attacking other communities; it is about asking whether Black political visibility is translating into ownership, contracts, land, trade skills, business development, institutional power, and wealth that can be passed forward.
